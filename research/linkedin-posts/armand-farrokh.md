### Content

Team selling boosts win rates by 50%.

But most team selling looks uncoordinated.

Instead, assign clear roles:

1️⃣ The Main Event  
→ runs the majority of the call  

2️⃣ The Storyteller  
→ uses stories to elevate and engage  

3️⃣ The Bad Guy  
→ handles tough conversations (pricing, objections)  

4️⃣ The Room Reader  
→ observes reactions and guides adjustments  

Two effective plays:

**Question Elevator**
→ teammate jumps in, asks deeper questions, then hands back control  

**Story Baton Pass**
→ handoff to another teammate for a story or insight  

---

### Insights

- Most sales teams fail due to **lack of coordination, not lack of skill**
- Clear role assignment increases efficiency and confidence during calls
- Team selling works when responsibilities are **predefined, not reactive**
- Strategic handoffs create smoother, more engaging conversations
- Sales is a **team performance**, not an individual effort

---

### Actionable Tactics

- Before every call:
  - assign roles clearly (who does what)
- Define:
  - who leads  
  - who supports  
  - who handles objections  
- Use structured handoffs:
  - “Let me bring in [name] for this”  
- Practice:
  - Question Elevator → deepen conversations  
  - Story Baton Pass → add context and credibility  
- Debrief after calls:
  - what worked  
  - where coordination broke  

---

### My Takeaways

- This adds **execution structure** to outbound and sales calls  
- Complements:
  - Berman → systems  
  - Braun → psychology  
  - Armand → real-time execution  
- For ModlyAI:
  → useful when doing demos or sales calls with partners/team  
- Key idea:
  → great sales calls are coordinated systems, not improvisation

### Content

Armand reflects on improving as a delegator and leader.

Instead of doing everything himself, he focuses on:

- Breaking work into milestones (“shuttle running”) instead of expecting a perfect final output  
- Treating mistakes as part of the learning process  
- Not expecting team members to read his mind  
- Reinforcing what went right, not just fixing what went wrong  
- Shifting from a “to-do list” to a “you-do list” (enabling the team)

Core idea:
Leadership is not about perfect execution — it’s about building systems and people who can execute.

---

### Insights

- Poor delegation often comes from **unclear expectations, not poor performance**
- Mistakes are required for skill development, not something to avoid
- Most execution failures are **communication failures**
- Teams scale when leaders focus on **enabling others, not doing everything**
- Reinforcing success is as important as correcting mistakes

---

### Actionable Tactics

- Break projects into checkpoints:
  - draft → review → iterate → finalize
- During delegation:
  - clearly define expected outcome + context
- When mistakes happen:
  - treat them as coaching moments, not failures
- Build feedback loops:
  - highlight what worked + what to improve
- Replace personal task lists with:
  - “What can my team accomplish today with my support?”

---

### My Takeaways

- This connects to outbound execution:
  → scaling outreach requires delegation, not solo effort  
- Complements Armand’s first post:
  → structure applies both to calls AND team workflows  
- For ModlyAI:
  → important when scaling operations or onboarding help  
- Key idea:
  → great execution comes from systems + people, not individual effort

### Content

Success in a new job starts before day one.

Instead of learning everything during onboarding:
- learn industry language early  
- study org structures  
- listen to relevant content  
- practice core skills (like cold calls)  

This allows you to:
→ use information instead of struggling to understand it  

Most people fall behind because they:
- don’t understand basic terms  
- spend onboarding catching up instead of improving  

Top performers:
→ arrive already 80% prepared  

---

### Insights

- Preparation creates a **massive early advantage**
- Lack of fundamentals blocks higher-level skill development  
- Top performers reduce onboarding friction before starting  
- Most people fall behind due to **information overload, not capability**
- Early preparation shifts focus from learning → execution  

---

### Actionable Tactics

- Before starting any role:
  - learn key terminology  
  - study industry basics  
- Build a “pre-onboarding plan”:
  - podcasts  
  - articles  
  - practice scripts  
- Practice core skills early:
  - cold calls  
  - messaging  
- Focus on:
  - understanding concepts → then applying them  
- Set a goal:
  → enter day one already ahead of the average  

---
