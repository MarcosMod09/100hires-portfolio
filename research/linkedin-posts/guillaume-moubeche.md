## Post 2

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/guillaume-moubeche-a026541b2_when-a-founder-tells-you-that-they-did-a-share-7268246251551260673-UIZt?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFzJJ1sBCA1W7yrq47kyaYtHMd3I5V5RJrU

### Content

A founder says they had a “7-figure exit.”

Sounds impressive.

But the reality looks like this:
- $1,000,000 sale  
- $100,000 broker fee  
- $50,000 legal fees  
- Split between 3 founders → ~$283k each  
- 20% tax → ~$227k  
- Minus $200k loan / unpaid salary  

Final result:
→ ~$27,000

The headline sounds big.
The outcome is very different.

---

### Insights

- Revenue and exit size can be **misleading without context**
- Net outcome matters more than headline numbers  
- Many founders underestimate costs:
  - fees  
  - taxes  
  - dilution  
- Entrepreneurship often looks better externally than internally  
- Financial clarity is critical when evaluating opportunities  

---

### Actionable Tactics

- When evaluating a business or offer:
  - focus on **net profit, not top-line numbers**
- Break down:
  - fees  
  - taxes  
  - ownership split  
- Track true earnings over time, not just exit value  
- Avoid decisions based on vanity metrics (revenue, valuation)  
- Prioritize:
  - profitability  
  - sustainable cash flow  

---

### My Takeaways

- This connects to outbound and growth:
  → more revenue doesn’t automatically mean better outcomes  
- Reinforces the importance of **profit-first thinking**  
- For ModlyAI:
  → focus on margins and real cash flow, not just growth metrics  
- Strong reminder:
  → always look at the full financial picture, not the headline

### Content

Startups don’t die because they fail.

They die because founders quit.

Not from lack of ideas or effort —
but from trying to survive on nothing for too long.

We’re told to:
- grind
- reinvest everything
- delay personal needs

But that creates a different risk:
→ founder exhaustion

A better approach:

Pay yourself first.

You can’t build long-term if you can’t survive short-term.

Like oxygen masks on airplanes:
→ take care of yourself first, then others.

---

### Insights

- Founder sustainability is a **hidden constraint in business growth**
- Burnout often comes from financial pressure, not workload  
- Over-reinvesting can reduce long-term execution quality  
- Survival is a prerequisite for growth  
- Personal stability directly affects business decisions  

---

### Actionable Tactics

- Set a baseline salary early:
  - enough to remove survival pressure  
- Balance:
  - reinvestment vs personal sustainability  
- Track:
  - runway for both business and personal finances  
- Avoid “all-in reinvestment” if it creates instability  
- Build systems that support long-term consistency  

---

### My Takeaways

- This complements the other experts by adding a **founder-level constraint**
- Connects directly to Berman’s constraint theory:
  → if the founder burns out, the business stops  
- For ModlyAI:
  → ensure sustainable income while building  
  → avoid sacrificing long-term execution for short-term growth  
- Key idea:
  → sustainable founders build sustainable businesses
