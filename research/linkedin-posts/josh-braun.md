## Post 1

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_you-send-a-cold-email-you-worked-hard-on-activity-7453448441495535619-SCy_?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFzJJ1sBCA1W7yrq47kyaYtHMd3I5V5RJrU

### Content

You send a cold email you worked hard on.

Tight message. Thoughtful. Feels right.

Then you wait.

Nothing.

Your mind starts talking:
- “They didn’t like it.”
- “I should’ve rewritten that line.”
- “Maybe I should bump it now.”

Detaching in that moment means:
You notice the urge to control the outcome — and you don’t act on it.

You pause and ask:
“Did I do my part well?”

If yes → you move on.

Because:
Effort is yours. Outcome isn’t.

---

### Insights

- Most outbound failure is **emotional, not technical**
- Founders over-optimize messaging instead of managing expectations
- Lack of response is often misinterpreted as rejection
- External factors (timing, inbox, priorities) heavily influence outcomes
- Consistency beats perfection in outbound

---

### Actionable Tactics

- After sending an email, evaluate only:
  → “Did I execute well?”
- Avoid immediate tweaking or second-guessing
- Build a system:
  - send → log → move on
- Separate:
  - effort (controllable)
  - outcome (uncontrollable)
- Use follow-ups strategically, not emotionally

---

### My Takeaways

- This complements Berman’s systems with **mental discipline**
- Outbound is not just messaging — it’s managing expectations and consistency
- For ModlyAI:
  → avoid overthinking each message  
  → focus on volume + iteration instead of perfection  
- Strong reminder:
  → success in outreach comes from process, not individual outcomes

## Post 2

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_picture-a-customer-deciding-whether-to-wait-activity-7453231825822486528-H37K?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFzJJ1sBCA1W7yrq47kyaYtHMd3I5V5RJrU

### Content

A customer is deciding whether to wait before buying a new car.

The current one still works.
But warning signs are there:
- strange noises
- check engine lights
- rising repair costs

They hesitate.

Not because they don’t want the new car —
but because they’re comparing **now vs later**.

In sales, this is where tension shows up:
- price feels high
- resistance increases

Not because the price is wrong —
but because the comparison is incomplete.

A better approach is reframing:

“If your payment goes up about $27, you’re handling reliability now.  
If you wait, that cost likely shows up later in repairs or a lower trade-in.  
How are you thinking about that?”

The goal is not to push the sale.

It’s to clarify the trade-off:
- known cost now  
- unknown cost later  

Then pause — and let them decide.

---

### Insights

- Price resistance is often a **comparison problem**, not a pricing problem  
- Buyers struggle more with **uncertainty** than with cost  
- Good sales conversations shift from persuasion → **decision clarity**  
- Tension increases when the buyer hasn’t fully processed trade-offs  
- Silence is a powerful tool after reframing  

---

### Actionable Tactics

- When price objections come up:
  - identify what the buyer is comparing (now vs later)
- Reframe the decision:
  - highlight **future cost vs present cost**
- Use neutral language:
  - avoid pushing or defending  
- Ask a guiding question:
  - “How are you thinking about that?”
- After reframing:
  - stop talking and let the buyer process  

---

### My Takeaways

- This complements outbound strategy with **closing psychology**
- Instead of pushing value, guide the buyer to see trade-offs clearly  
- For ModlyAI:
  → frame pricing around cost of not solving the problem (missed conversions, returns)  
- Strong reminder:
  → great sales isn’t convincing — it’s helping customers make clear decisions

## Post 3

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_youre-one-click-away-a-watch-a-jacket-activity-7453121418952859648-PW54?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFzJJ1sBCA1W7yrq47kyaYtHMd3I5V5RJrU

### Content

You’re one click away from buying something.

A watch. A jacket. Something you didn’t need five minutes ago.

You feel urgency.

“Buy now.”

So you act.

Relief — for a moment.
Then it fades.
Another craving appears.

Instead of stopping the craving, you observe it.

If you don’t act, it fades.

Now apply this to sales.

You’re about to send:
- “Just checking in”
- “Bumping this up”
- “Did you see my proposal?”

Same pattern.

Craving.

Needing the deal to move.
Needing a response.

So you act.

And prospects don’t feel your product.

They feel your **need**.

That creates resistance.

A better approach:
- notice the urge  
- don’t react immediately  
- let it pass  

Not every impulse needs action.

---

### Insights

- Desperation in sales is often **felt, not seen**
- Buyers react more to emotional signals than to the actual message
- Urgency from the seller creates resistance in the buyer
- Many follow-ups are driven by anxiety, not strategy
- Emotional control directly impacts conversion

---

### Actionable Tactics

- Before sending a follow-up, pause and ask:
  → “Is this strategic or emotional?”
- Avoid reactive messages like:
  - “just checking in”
  - “bumping this up”
- Create a follow-up system:
  - scheduled timing  
  - value-driven messages  
- Delay action when urgency feels high
- Focus on calm, intentional communication

---

### My Takeaways

- This completes Braun’s theme:
  → emotional discipline in outbound and sales  
- Strong connection to earlier post:
  → detach from outcome AND from urgency  
- For ModlyAI:
  → avoid desperate follow-ups  
  → focus on structured, value-based outreach  
- Key idea:
  → the best salespeople control their emotions, not just their messaging
